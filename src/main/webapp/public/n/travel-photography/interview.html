Travel Photography Tips ––
from the Experts
Ever wanted to improve your travel photography but weren't sure where to start? We interview Australia and New Zealand's top photographers on their tips for amateur photographers, their best kept secret photographic destinations, what's in their camera bag and more.

An Interview with Australia and New Zealand's Leading Photographers

  • Chris McLennan

    Copyright © Chris McLennan - Papua New Guinea

    1  What's your ultimate travel photography destination?
    Anywhere new :) Preferably with wildlife, mountains or primitive cultures. I do find myself returning to Alaska and Papua New Guinea.

    2  What in your opinion is Australia / New Zealand's best kept secret photographic gem?
    The Glenorchy board walk, it leaves right from the quaint township of Glenorchy at the head of lake Wakatipu and provides spectacular views and alpine reflections in all directions.

    3  What camera equipment do you take on the road?
    Nikon D8000, Nikon 14-24, 24 -70, 70-200 and macro lens. Other equipment is decided dependant on the location and subjects that I am shooting.

    4  What's your number 1 tip for amateur travel photographers?
    Stay passionate, follow your dreams and don't be afraid to create your own unique style. Everyone learns from their mistakes.

    5  How do you store your photo's while on the road?
    On a high capacity laptop and also several external hard drives.

    6  What's the most exciting photography trend of 2013?
    I don't tend to follow too many trends… For me the most exciting path for 2013 is leading more photo tours in Alaska and Africa allowing me to share the passion for photography with other like minded people.

    7  What's your favourite photograph of your own? tell us about the story behind it.
    I have so many and so many memorable stories, it's really hard to single one out… At the moment I am feeling passionate about wildlife photography. I have many favourites but one subject that always gets a lot of response is my images of breaching great white sharks in South Africa. The sheer power of these huge sharks as they breach in pursuit of seals is like something from a Sci-fi movie, especially when they are so close…. And then there are elephants'…

    8  What's the most inspiring thing you have photographed?
    Again its hard to choose individual subjects but one of them would have to be the primitive cultures of Papua New Guinea (featured above).

    9  What's your favourite travel photography blog?
    I don't have a lot of time to follow travel blogs due to the nature of my work. I do have my own one which is run by my wife which is a constant update of what we are up to with images, tips and tricks: BeyondF8

    Chris McLennan, an international award winning photographer who specialises in the travel, tourism and adventure industries world-wide.
    Chris's assignments take him all over the globe - shooting exotic travel lifestyles, forgotten cultures, world renowned landmarks, 5-star travel destinations, adventure products and much, much more - assignments that have seen him photograph in over 40 different countries to date!
    A recognised ambassador for his industry, Chris holds endorsement relationships with some of photography's top brands. His work is published internationally and his images have been recognised with a number of prestigious awards.

  • Ken Duncan

    Copyright © Ken Duncan - Atlantis Falls, WA

    1  What's your ultimate travel photography destination?
    Hill Inlet on Whitsunday Island is one of the most beautiful areas to photograph in the world.

    2  What in your opinion is Australia / New Zealand's best kept secret photographic gem?
    There is a waterfall in The Kimberley called Atlantis Falls. If I told you exactly where it was, it would no longer be a secret. If people really want to find it, they will. But I believe some places should be kept secret, as it's often the effort and adventure of getting to a location that gives it significance.

    3   What camera equipment do you take on the road?
    My main camera is a Phase One system with a P280 digital back. I also still shoot on film sometimes and my film camera is a Linhof 617S. For candid and quick shots, I now use the new Lumix GX7.

    4  What's your number 1 tip for amateur travel photographers?
    Be patient. Great photos are gifts that come when you spend time relating to people or the location you're in.

    5  How do you store your photo's while on the road?
    I carry 2 portable hard drives, which I keep in 2 separate locations. One is usually in my luggage and the other is in my camera case or computer case. That way, if one drive is lost or stolen, I still have all the images backed up.

    6   What's the most exciting photography trend of 2013?
    The new Lumix micro 4/3 camera – the GX7 – is a game changer for the photographic industry. It has extremely high quality and capability for shooting in very low light – for both stills and video.

    7  What's your favourite photograph of your own? tell us about the story behind it.
    My favourite photo – and one that has become quite famous – is a shot called the Power of One. I was on safari in Zimbabwe at a magical spot called Mana Pools. This huge bull elephant walked right past our camp and down to the river, then swam out to the grassy islands in the middle of the mighty Zambesi. Our guide thought this might be a good opportunity for me to get close with my very wide angle camera, so we paddled out into the river and slowly moved closer to the big bull. We kept a watchful eye for crocodiles and hippos, which are plentiful in this area. In the distance, you can actually see hippopotamus on the river bank. This elephant was only about 30 feet from us but my camera makes him appear further away. You will notice his left ear is slightly blurred – that's because it was twitching. Just as I took this shot, the elephant picked up our scent and began to charge. Fortunately for my racing heart, he stopped after only a few paces. He is an awesome sight - a great elephant in the mighty Zambesi River.

    8   What's the most inspiring thing you have photographed?
    The most inspiring photography I have ever done was on the set of The Passion of the Christ movie with Mel Gibson. The sets were so well researched and realistic that it was like being transported back to the time of Christ.

    Ken Duncan, multi award-winning artist and OAM recipient, is recognised internationally as a gifted and prolific landscape photographer. A national TV current affairs program described Ken as an "iconic Australian image maker" and a leading American gallery described him as "the Ansel Adams of colour photography". His Limited Edition Art Prints grace the walls of fine homes and office suites right around the world. Ken, however, describes himself as an average photographer, with a great God ... merely an interpreter of God's creation. There is no doubt that God has given him a great gift to capture and present the splendour of His creation.
    Considered a master in his field of photography, Ken has won numerous awards both nationally and internationally for his images and publications – including the photographic industry's highest honour – the coveted Gold Tripod Award.

  • Everlook Photography

    Copyright © Marianne Lim & Dylan Toh - Lake Mackenzie, NZ

    1  What's your ultimate travel photography destination?
    Iceland!

    2  What in your opinion is Australia / New Zealand's best kept secret photographic gem?
    In Australia, much of the Eyre Peninsula remains relatively unphotographed. The granite rock formations , in particular Murphy's haystacks are one of my favourite. In New Zealand, one of my favourite hikes was up to the summit of Mount Roy. On a clear day, the visibility stretches beyond the end of Lake Wanaka and into Mount Aspiring National Park.

    3  What camera equipment do you take on the road?
    We take a 5dmk2, 5dmk3 and a 7d as a spare. Wide angle lenses include 16-35mm F2.8 II lens and 17-40mm F4 lens. We bring a 70-200mm F2.8 II lens and a 135mm F2 lens. As a walkaround lens outside of landscapes, we also carry a 24-70mm F2.8 lens. We carry a large assortment of filters including circular polarisers and GND filters. We also have two carbon fibre tripods.

    4  What's your number 1 tip for amateur travel photographers?
    The most important tip is to give yourself ample opportunity to photograph locations. I think it is better to spend more time at one place rather than spending most of your time travelling and feeling fatigued.

    5  How do you store your photo's while on the road?
    We store our images on a laptop as well as an external hard drive.

    6  What's the most exciting photography trend of 2013?
    The most exciting growth in photography has been that of social media. In many ways, this has made it easy to make any given photographer's work available to the public. This has meant that there is an absolute increase in the number of quality images out there, but the flipside is that there is probably relatively less quality images due to the sheer volume of average work.

    7  What's your favourite photograph of your own? tell us about the story behind it.
    One of our favourite photographs is Marianne's image of Korpudalur in Iceland. At the end of a long Icelandic summer day on the road, we witnessed rays of horizontal light for an extended period of twilight transform the West Fjords into an even more magical place.

    8  What's the most inspiring thing you have photographed?
    The northern lights (aurora borealis). Coming from the southern hemisphere, we rarely see anything even resembling this phenomenon and when we finally witnessed it for the first time, it had us wanting to return in winter for more!

    9  What's your favourite travel photography blog?
    PhotoBotos.com

    Marianne Lim and Dylan Toh are both self-taught amateur photographers who are based in Adelaide , South Australia. Our love of photography grew from a desire to capture lasting impressions of our travels. As working professionals, we rely on photography to escape the routines of the working week. As new parents, we also hope to share the experience of nature with our child and hope that this contributes to the development of her own sense of adventure. We hope to convey a sense of awe, mood and appreciation through our photographs. We are ever in search of that one perfect moment, that one perfect scene which will give us that one perfect photograph. Walking paths both near and far will hopefully give us many more opportunities in the future to capture and share these scenes.

  • Drew Hopper

    Copyright © Drew Hopper - Dorrigo National Park, NSW

    1  What's your ultimate travel photography destination?
    Isn't it every travel photographers ultimate photography destination the World? I simply cannot choose just one location, my list is endless and until I scratch the service I don't have an ultimate travel destination.

    2  What in your opinion is Australia / New Zealand's best kept secret photographic gem?
    In my eyes the lush enchanted rainforest found along the East Coast of Australia is the most precious best kept secret from a photographers perspective. These wonderful places are untouched from the human destruction that is overdeveloping the world and holds great significance to life on Earth. I'd have to say the same for New Zealand's luscious forests.

    3  What camera equipment do you take on the road?
    Canon 5D Mark II
    Canon 16-35 2.8L
    Canon 24-70 2.8L
    Canon 70-200 2.8L
    Sigma 35 1.4
    MeFoto Roadtrip Travel Tripod/Monopod
    Kata 3N1-33 Backpack/Sling Bag
    Lee & Hitech Graduated ND Filters
    64GB Memory Cards
    1TB Western Digital Passport Hard Drive
    Macbook Pro 13" Laptop

    4  What's your number 1 tip for amateur travel photographers?
    I have learnt to avoid the technique known as 'chimping', or in other words shooting an image and immediately reviewing it on the cameras LCD screen. Most DSLR cameras playback/review time is between 1-3 seconds and this timing can be crucial if photographing a high speed event and and constantly waiting to review your shot before firing your next frame. A better shot could be missed while you examine your previous photo. I now shoot more and review less to avoid missing any of the action.

    5  How do you store your photo's while on the road?
    All my images are imported straight onto my Macbook Pro's internal 1TB drive and then backed up onto an external portable 1TB for extra security. This portable drive is kept offsite to be sure it's safe.

    6  What's the most exciting photography trend of 2013?
    'Within the past few years, mirrorless and Micro Four Thirds cameras have made their stamp on the industry and stolen attention from both the point and shoot and DSLR markets. With smaller sensors than their DSLR counterparts and more capable systems than entry-level cameras, they've become what're called the next-gen.'

    7  What's your favourite photograph of your own? tell us about the story behind it.
    The picture above I titled 'Mystic Jungle' would be my personal favourite. The bold and central composition is eye catching which has made it my most popular and recognised photo to date. Taken deep in World Heritage Dorrigo National Park - NSW, Australia. It was the perfect day for a trek in the forest, just after the rain had eased and the sun begun to shine from through the canopy. I still remember the excitement felt when seeing it on my cameras LCD screen for the first time, I knew I had created something special that would connect with people of all levels. It has been published worldwide in magazines, postcards, websites and only blogs everywhere.

    8  What's the most inspiring thing you have photographed?
    Tough Question, I cannot really answer it to be honest. My most inspiring photography trip was to Sydney in Sept 2012. It was a spontaneous trip to photograph some new territory which ended up rewarding me with some incredibly successful pictures that have scored me contracts with tourism companies in Australia.

    9  What's your favourite travel photography blog?
    Lonely Planet's best-selling travel photography, internationally renowned travel photographer Richard I'Anson would have to be my travel shooting inspiration. Richard's work is world class with many publications around the globe.

    Drew Hopper is an internationally renowned fine art travelling landscape photographer with a passion for the natural world. Captivated by the diversity of the Australian landscape, he travels far and wide to capture photographs that define his experiences, encapsulate those raw moments, and share Australia's most extraordinary places with the world. Drew's work is published in magazines, newspapers, books, posters, cards, and on web pages around the globe. Drew's bold eye for composition and light have seen him capture some award winning photos, which reflect his unique style.

  • Todd & Sarah Sisson

    Copyright © Todd & Sarah Sisson - Yellow Eyed Penguin, NZ

    1  What's your ultimate travel photography destination?
    We have both always dreamed of doing an Alaskan RV mission from Spring until Autumn - but after 10 days with the kids in this campervan we might be holding that dream back for another 10 years or so.... a Fijian island with Kid's Club facilities would seem to offer plenty of photographic possibilities.

    2  What in your opinion is Australia / New Zealand's best kept secret photographic gem?
    The whole South Island of New Zealand! There always seems to be another ridiculously photogenic spot around the corner - I'm writing this from a campervan parked next to a stunning west coast beach that I have never heard of, in fact, I doubt it even has a name. Sarah & I spent the morning photographing this beach and we know for a fact that around the headland lies another stunning coastal location. To single out one stupidly great spot over another simply seems unfair, if not impossible!

    3  What camera equipment do you take on the road?
    For landscape photography you really only need two or three lenses, a DSLR body and a tripod. Of course Sarah & I never stick to this (because we also photograph people for our stock library during our travels). Between the two of us, things rapidly spiral out of control - no matter the size of vehicle there is always stuff cascading out of doors and jammed under seats - our 6 year old daughter just had our son's compact camera bag fall on her head this morning thanks to some poor parental packing skills!

    4  What's your number 1 tip for amateur travel photographers?
    Stay amateur. Let your boss worry about tax returns, bank managers, clients, marketing and bizarre government regulations. Just build a website that looks like you are a pro and sell your images through the myriad of channels available on the web. Good stock agencies are always on the lookout for great images and they don't care whether you are a grizzled full time photographer or a radiator hose pipe salesman - it is the quality of your images that counts. If after two years of this you find yourself lighting campfires with $100 bills then it may be time to go full time - that should quickly remedy your excess cash issues...

    5  How do you store your photo's while on the road?
    Haphazardly! Sarah & I grew up on film and the digital transition was an iterative process so we have never really formalised a system. Having said that, we are borderline paranoids, so images get downloaded to a laptop, then backed up to USB-powered hard drive. We don't re-use or erase the camera memory cards until we get back to the office, meaning we have three copies spread around the vehicle (the USB hard drive gets hidden somewhere in case of a break-in). On big trips we will courier another hard drive back to the office.

    6  What's the most exciting photography trend of 2013?
    Bellbottoms? shoulder pads? I'll leave the trends to the vespa-riding Instagram hipsters of Greenwich Village - I'm simply too old, uncool and lazy to be worried about trends. Great photography is about unchanging fundamentals, composition, subject matter and, most of all, soul-stirringly great light.

    7  What's your favourite photograph of your own? tell us about the story behind it.
    This one of a clearing storm over Mitre Peak in Milford Sound. I just love this image, as it represents this incredible place in a way that I feel encapsulates how I felt when looking at the scene - it is brooding, dark yet calm and comfortable - to me, it has 'soul'. Even better, it came about easily - I had been having a little snooze in the back of our SUV (after an early start - I swear) when my alarm went off. After a frenzied stagger down to the beach, I snapped a few dozen images and then the light fizzled - I returned to the car and hit the sack again, a perfect day!

    8  What's the most inspiring thing you have photographed?
    I would love to say the birth of my two children - but I failed spectacularly on both occasions (a career in war photography was not to be). Instead, it would be a summer evening spent with the Yellow eyed penguins at Katiki Point near Moeraki, Otago. There were a few people about but they all left to have their dinner I guess, because suddenly I was alone on the headland with these beautiful, and incredibly rare, birds wandering past me - it was surreal. For this shot I just lay in the grass and let this penguin walk on by - he or she stopped for a while to check me out and then left - a spiritual moment...

    9  What's your favourite travel photography blog? Please include the URL.
    He doesn't need the extra traffic, but Trey Ratcliff at Stuck in Customs is the consummate travel photography blogger.

    Todd & Sarah Sisson are New Zealand based professional landscape photographers. They are the authors of a top-selling instructional eBook, Living Landscapes - a guide to stunning landscape photography, and their work has appeared internationally in publications, marketing collateral and as fine art prints.

  • Highlux Photo

    Copyright © Mark Watson - Shangri La (Zhongdian) Yunnan, China

    1  What's your ultimate travel photography destination?
    Any place that has a vibrant (preferably ancient) culture, geographical diversity, interesting history and friendly people. South East Asia is my current favourite. India and South and Central America are high on my list of places to visit.

    2  What in your opinion is Australia / New Zealand's best kept secret photographic gem?
    Well that would be telling... New Zealand's full of locations that have scarcely been photographed and one only has to get off the beaten track a little to find beautiful, lesser known places. The West Coast of the South Island for one has many seldom walked valleys.

    3  What camera equipment do you take on the road?
    Canon 5D MKII, plus a 5DMKI as a spare. Canon 16-35mm f2.8L, Canon 24-70mm f2.8L, Sigma 70-200mm 2.8L, Sigma 15mm fisheye, plus tripod and filters. If I'm cycling I'll just carry one body and a Canon 24-105mm f4L plus a fisheye or a 50mm f1.8, and a light tripod.

    4  What's your number 1 tip for amateur travel photographers?
    Keep your camera as handy as possible and keep your kit simple and minimal. If it feels like a burden you're less likely to take it with you everywhere.

    5  How do you store your photo's while on the road?
    I upload via Adobe Lightroom onto a 500GB USB-powered hard drive and then back up to a second hard drive. Processed and finished shots are uploaded to Smugmug as I travel.

    6  What's the most exciting photography trend of 2013?
    Mirrorless body cameras. They're so much lighter and more compact. These are the future of professional level portable camera systems.

    7  What's your favourite photograph of your own? tell us about the story behind it.
    A shot of a butcher, surrounded by animal carcasses that I took in a market in Shangri La (Zhongdian) in Yunnan, China while on a 13,000 km cycle tour through SE Asia. It's a simple, tightly framed shot that renders a scene that is typical for a market in China, but so very different to something you'd see in Western culture.

    8  What's the most inspiring thing you have photographed?
    Probably the Angkor temples in Cambodia, but photographing action sports such as climbing can have some inspiring moments too.

    9  What's your favourite travel photography blog?
    Steve McCurry's travel work is a huge inspiration. He has a gifted eye for both composition and moment, and the ability to find beauty in the mundane.

    Mark Watson is a freelance outdoor photographer based in Lyttelton in New Zealand's South Island. He shoots landscape, mountain recreation and travel for both editorial and commercial clients. Mark's work has been widely published in books, magazines (feature, editorial and advertising), catalogues and online, and he is a regular contributor to both The Climber and Wilderness Magazine. Mark's first book Our Mountains (co-authored with Paul Hersey) celebrating 15 of New Zealand's most significant mountains is due out in October.

    Copyright © Alex Wise - Bay of Fires, TAS

    1bbsp; What's your ultimate travel photography destination?

  • Penelope Beveridge

    Copyright © Penelope Beveridge - The Olgas, NT

    1  What's your ultimate travel photography destination?
    My ultimate travel photography is above the Earth. The world looks very different from the air, the colours, the topography of the landscape, the layouts of towns all take a more abstract viewpoint. One of my favourite places in the world for aerial photography is here in Australia, over Whitehaven Beach, Whitsunday Islands Queensland.

    2  What in your opinion is Australia / New Zealand's best kept secret photographic gem?
    Western Australia's vast coastline is heaven to travel and landscape photographers. Pick any spot from Esperance to Kununurra and you will not be disappointed. The most spectacular sunset and sunrise I have ever seen was at Monkey Mia with dolphins swimming up next to me. For an unusual landscape photo that feels out of this world's check out "The Pinnacles" which are limestone formations contained within Nambung National Park, near the town of Cervantes.

    3  What camera equipment do you take on the road?
    A tripod is absolutely essential but if the weight is too heavy I will use a mini tripod, which is not as sturdy but serves the purpose of slow shutter speed and night photography. My photography backpack always has 2 camera bodies and my main camera is generally Nikon D800E camera body with a minimum of 3 lenses Nikon 14-24mm, 70-200mm, 24-70mm. If I have the room I will add 105mm macro lens for close-ups and if I wish to shoot video my 50mm f18 will be tucked away with the gear too. My MacBook Pro computer is another integral part of the kit and a hard drive to transfer and store my raw files. Finally, I always take my mobile phone as I can also include excellent quality phone images as well as the high resolution camera files, that can be shared on numerous social media sites and instantly uploaded.

  • Andy Belcher

    Copyright © Andy Belcher - Whale Shark, Vava'u, Tonga

    1  What's your ultimate travel photography destination?
    Antarctica.

    2  What in your opinion is Australia / New Zealand's best kept secret photographic gem?
    The Blue Lake at St Bathans, Central Otago, South Island.

    3  What camera equipment do you take on the road?
    It depends on the shoot. If it is a full commercial shoot I will take my pro bag which contains a Nikon D3, 14-24, 24-70, 70-200mm F2.8 Nikon lenses plus an old 16mm fisheye. Also SB800 flash, cable releases, remotes, tripod etc.
    For a quick lightweight kit I use a Panasonic Lumix GF2 with a gorgeous 7-14mm F4 zoom lens. I also have a Recsea housing for this camera so I can use it underwater. I will often also take a Go Pro Hero 2 and use it on a Camera pole.

    4  What's your number 1 tip for amateur travel photographers?
    Always carry a spare fully charged camera battery. Have your camera always set on Aperture priority at F8 and the ISO set at ISO 400. These settings will usually enable you to grab the camera, switch it on and shoot immediately with a fast enough shutter speed to match your lens. All you need to concentrate on is composing the image and selecting the best viewpoint. Be able to recognize a great photo and never miss your best opportunity to capture it.

    5  How do you store your photo's while on the road?
    My Nikon D3 has two slots for compact flash cards. This enable me to use two 64 GB cards, enough to shoot for two weeks without storing images elsewhere. Make photography as simple as possible is my philosophy.

    6  What's the most exciting photography trend of 2013?
    The development and constant improvement of mirrorless compact cameras. They are so small, light, easy to use and give amazing results. I dont really follow other people's photo trends. I try to create photo trends of my own by thinking outside the square! I like people to react to my photos with comments like "wow, how did you do that?"

    7  What's your favourite photograph of your own? tell us about the story behind it.
    My favourite photo is of a whale shark in Vava'u, Tonga. On day four, after a number of hours searching, we struggled to find a good whale encounter. Moa, our female skipper received a call from another boat and we were soon alongside it. "Get ready" she called to us. I looked around and could not see a humpback anywhere in sight. "Where is the whale?" I asked. She quickly responded, "Trust me. Swim that way. GO, GO, GO". I slid gently into the water, camera in hand. The boat's propeller had caused aerated water like a fog and I swam blindly through it. When I emerged into clear water I was greeted by an enormous whale shark, only 3 or 4 metres away and coming straight at me! I tried to gather my senses and back peddled furiously to one side to avoid a head on collision with this monster. It slid past me very close and all I could see was white spots! Even though the fish appeared to swim very slowly there was no way of keeping up with it. We returned to the boat, moved ahead of it and then jumped in again. This time I was ready. ISO 320 and an aperture of F8 gave me a shutter speed of 1/180th second. Even with the Tokina lens at its widest setting of 11mm I had to move back to get the whole whale shark in the picture area.
    At 12 to 14 metres the whale shark is the world's largest fish and seeing a snorkeler behind it gives you an appreciation of how big it really is. We swam beside it for a whole 90 minutes in water which was 1000 metres deep with visibility of 70 metres plus. It doesn't get much better than that! I have been diving since 1973 and this was without doubt my best encounter ever and beyond my wildest dreams.

    8  What's the most inspiring thing you have photographed?
    My son holding my newly born grandson.
